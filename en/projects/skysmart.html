Context

Skysmart is the K-12 product of Skyeng, Russia's largest online school. English, math, Russian language, exam preparation. One-on-one lessons with tutors and self-paced exercises.

I worked as a product designer on the Learning Experience team. Designed the interfaces students use every day: dashboard, progress tracking, goal setting, course navigation.

2

My role

Product designer. Four areas of work:

  • Student dashboard — daily tasks, schedule, subject navigation
  • Student progress — learning metrics, onboarding, recommendations, achievements
  • Goal setting — level diagnostics, goal selection, cabinet widgets
  • Course map — visual learning path
3

Student dashboard

The main page students see after logging in. Daily tasks, schedule, upcoming lesson, subject switching.

Designed multiple versions: for one-on-one lessons (F2F), group sessions (F2G), and self-paced learning on the Skysmart platform. Each format has different priorities — F2F focuses on schedule and tutor contact, Skysmart emphasizes tasks and course progress.

Additionally: subscription packages, to-do lists, exam practice tabs, contact widget, webinar dates. Every feature — desktop and mobile.

4

Student progress

A page that shows how things are going. Not just "completed 5 of 20 lessons" — a "health" system that combines engagement, discipline, and academic performance into one picture.

Four student states: current level, recommendations, subject health, analytics. Each has its own screen and widget set. Teachers see the same data from their side — to understand where the student needs help.

Onboarding in four steps: invitation, selection, setup, first result. Prototypes were tested with real students and teachers.

5

Goal setting

A system that helps students answer "why am I learning this." Level diagnostics, goal selection, progress tracking toward it.

Starts with the CJM: student arrives for the first lesson, takes a diagnostic test, chooses a goal in their cabinet. Three subjects — Kids English, Kids Math, Adults English. Each has its own goal set and widget design.

The goal widget in the cabinet goes through multiple states: no goal set, survey partially completed, goal selected, new activities on the course map. Each state — a separate design with CTA and feedback.

6

Design approach

Every feature went through stages: draft, WIP, development, production, archive. The files contain the full history of iterations, from first sketches to final screens.

UX research was built into the process. For student progress and teacher views — separate prototypes for testing. Test results fed back into design.

Component-driven: goal widgets, lesson cards, navigation — all built from reusable components. Desktop and mobile designed in parallel.

7

Scale

Four design workstreams. Over 20 Figma pages. Hundreds of component iterations. Desktop and mobile versions of every screen.

Interfaces used by students aged 4 to 18 — each with its own constraints: simple language and large elements for younger kids, serious tone for exam preparation.
